Analysis of Foreign Exchange Rates

Downloading Exchange rate data in R

Firstly we need to download the exchange rate data. We can download this data for free from Yahoo Finance. The quantmod package provides an api to access financial data from various providers such as Yahoo Finance, Federal Reserve Economic Database (FRED), Alpha Vantage, Oanda and Tiingo.

Here, I will use Yahoo Finance and Alpha Vantage to download historical rates for the United States Dollar (USD) to the Zambian Kwacha (ZMK/ZMW), USD to South African Rand (ZAR) and ZAR to ZMW.

I want to analyse the performance of the Zambian Kwacha against the US Dollar since 2010. Since the Zambian Kwacha was rebased in 2013, I need to download two price series: one for ZMK, from 2010 to 2012, and one for ZMW, from 2013 to present.

Now we can combine the two series. First thing to do is to convert zmk to an xts object, so that it’s of the same class as zmw. The tk_xts function from the timetk package will use the date column in zmk as the index of the time series object and will drop non numeric columns, which in this case is the symbol column. Now zmk has the same number of columns as zmw.

zmk <- tk_xts(zmk)

Now we can combine the zmk and zmw series.

zmw <- rbind.xts(zmk, zmw)
zmw <- na.fill(zmw, "extend")

Now let us look at the exchange rate chart.

highchart(type = "stock") %>% 
  hc_title(text = "USD/ZMW Exchange Rate") %>%
  hc_add_series(zmw) %>% 
  hc_add_theme(my_theme) %>%
  hc_exporting(enabled = TRUE, scale = 4) %>% 
  hc_credits(enabled = TRUE, text = "Source: Yahoo Finance")

The chart above is called a candlestick chart. The length of the bar (called a candle) shows the size of the movement in that month. For example, the exchange rate opened below K9 when September 2015 trading began and closed above K12 at the end of the month.

The line shows (called the stick; hence: candlestick) shows the highest rate in the month. But this is not necessarily the last rate of the month. For example, in November 2015, rate opened above K12 in the beginning of the month, went as high as K14 during the month and closed at K10.3 at the end of the month.

The chart above seems to suggest that most of the depreciation of the Kwacha against the dollar has come after 2014.

The chart above shows the daily percentage changes in the USD/ZMW exchange rate. Since this is the quote of the amount of Kwacha that purchases 1 US Dollar, a positive change indicates a depreciation of the Kwacha. Conversely, a negative change indicates that the Kwacha has appreciated.

The bits highlighted in red show periods of high volatility. These periods are highlighted because the volatility in the last 21 days is higher than the average volatility of the series. The graph shows that periods of instability in the Kwacha have been more frequent, and of greater length, post 2014. This confirms the observation from the earlier candlestick graph, which indicated larger monthly movements in the Kwacha post 2014.
